## 1. FATF / CFATF Status

### Current FATF Listing Status

**The Dominican Republic is NOT on the FATF grey list or black list as of February 2026.**

- The February 13, 2026 FATF grey list (Jurisdictions under Increased Monitoring) includes: Algeria, Angola, Bolivia, Bulgaria, Cameroon, Cote d'Ivoire, DRC, Haiti, Kenya, Kuwait, Lao PDR, Lebanon, Monaco, Namibia, Nepal, Papua New Guinea, South Sudan, Syria, Venezuela, Vietnam, British Virgin Islands, and Yemen. DR is not listed.
- The FATF black list (High-Risk Jurisdictions Subject to a Call for Action) includes only: North Korea, Iran, and Myanmar. DR is not listed.

**Source:** [FATF -- Jurisdictions under Increased Monitoring (Feb 2026)](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/High-risk-and-other-monitored-jurisdictions/increased-monitoring-february-2026.html)

## 2. US State Department INCSR

### Presidential Determination -- Major Drug Transit Country

The Dominican Republic has been designated as a **Major Drug Transit Country** under the Presidential Determination for FY2026 (issued September 8, 2025). This designation has been applied consistently for many years.

**Source:** [State Dept -- Presidential Determination FY2026](https://www.state.gov/releases/office-of-the-spokesperson/2025/09/presidential-determination-on-major-drug-transit-or-major-illicit-drug-producing-countries-for-fiscal-year-2026)

### INCSR Volume II -- Money Laundering

The Dominican Republic is listed as a **jurisdiction of concern** in the INCSR Volume II (Money Laundering and Financial Crimes). Key findings from the INCSR reports:

- DR is classified as a "major money laundering country" -- defined as one whose financial institutions engage in currency transactions involving significant amounts of proceeds from international narcotics trafficking.
- Corruption within the government and private sector, the presence of international illicit trafficking cartels, a large informal economy, and weak financial controls make the DR vulnerable to money laundering and terrorism financing.
- Currency exchange houses, remittance companies, real estate and construction companies, and casinos facilitate laundering of illicit funds.
- Bulk cash smuggling by couriers and wire transfer remittances are primary methods for moving illicit funds from the US into DR.

**Source:** [2024 INCSR Vol 2 -- Money Laundering (State Dept PDF)](https://www.state.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/2024-INCSR-Vol-2-Money-Laundering-Accessible-Version.pdf)
**Source:** [2025 INCSR Report](https://www.state.gov/2025-international-narcotics-control-strategy-report)

## 5. Legal Framework

### Key AML Laws

#### Law 155-17 (2017) -- Anti-Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing Act
- **Enacted:** June 1, 2017
- **Status:** Primary and current AML/CFT law
- **Replaces:** Law 72-02 of 2002 (which focused narrowly on drug-related money laundering)
- **Scope:** Significantly broader than predecessor; establishes 30+ predicate offenses including:
  - Financial crimes, bribery, cross-border bribery
  - Extortion, tax evasion, tax-related infractions
  - Insider trading, market manipulation
  - Hi-tech/cyber crimes
  - Offenses committed by public officials
  - Environmental infractions
- **Key Requirements:**
  - Suspicious Transaction Reporting (STR) obligations for financial institutions, businesses, and designated professionals
  - Cash Transaction Reports (CTR) for transactions exceeding **USD $10,000**
  - Customer Due Diligence (CDD) and ongoing monitoring
  - Enhanced Due Diligence for PEPs and high-risk activities
  - Beneficial ownership identification requirements
  - Real estate brokers, lawyers, notaries, and accountants brought under AML obligations

#### Law 72-02 (2002) -- Former AML Law (Repealed)
- Focused narrowly on laundering proceeds from illegal drug trafficking
- Replaced by Law 155-17 due to limited scope

#### Other Relevant Legislation
- **Law 137-03** -- Law on Human Smuggling and Trafficking (recently amended to remove requirement to prove force/fraud/coercion for child victims)
- **DNCD Law** -- Establishes the National Directorate for the Control of Drugs

**Source:** [National Law Review -- DR's New AML Act](https://natlawreview.com/article/dominican-republic-s-new-anti-money-laundering-and-terrorist-financing-act)
**Source:** [Guzman Ariza -- Law No. 155-17](https://drlawyer.com/new-dominican-money-laundering-law-no-155-17/)
**Source:** [Gadax -- Law 155-17](https://gadaxrd.com/en/ley-155-17-lavado-de-activos/)
**Source:** [Sanction Scanner -- AML in Dominican Republic](https://www.sanctionscanner.com/aml-guide/anti-money-laundering-aml-in-dominican-republic-604)

### Financial Intelligence Unit (UAF)

**Full Name:** Unidad de Analisis Financiero (UAF) -- Financial Analysis Unit

- Created under Law 155-17
- Functions as the national center for **reception, analysis, and communication** of financial intelligence
- Reports to a multi-agency committee (Comite Nacional contra el Lavado de Activos y el Financiamiento del Terrorismo)
- Responsibilities:
  - Receive and analyze STRs and CTRs
  - Request additional financial information from reporting entities
  - Disseminate intelligence to law enforcement
  - Coordinate with international counterparts (Egmont Group membership)

#### Institutional Framework -- National Committee Members
The AML/CFT National Committee is composed of:
1. **Treasury Minister** (President of the Committee)
2. Minister of Defence
3. Superintendent of Banks (SIB)
4. Superintendent of Securities
5. Attorney General of the Republic
6. DNCD President (National Directorate for the Control of Drugs)
7. President of the National Council on Drugs
8. **Director of the UAF** (Technical Secretary of the Committee)

**Source:** [Fundacion BBVA -- AML/TF Law](https://www.fundacionmicrofinanzasbbva.org/revistaprogreso/en/anti-money-laundering-and-financing-of-terrorism-law/)

## 6. Known Vulnerabilities

### 6.1 Cash-Intensive Economy

- The Dominican Republic operates a **highly cash-intensive economy**, particularly outside major urban centers.
- Cash is the dominant payment method in informal commerce, small retail, street vending, and agricultural transactions.
- Bulk cash smuggling by couriers is identified as a **primary method** for moving illicit funds from the US into the DR.
- The high volume of cash transactions makes it difficult to detect structuring, smurfing, and integration of illicit funds.

**Source:** [INCSR -- Dominican Republic country narrative](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/inl/rls/nrcrpt/2016/vol2/253396.htm)
**Source:** [MoneyLaundering.com -- Dominican Republic](https://www.moneylaundering.com/countries/dominican-republic/)

### 6.2 Informal Economy / Unbanked Population

- **54.7% of total employment** is in the informal sector (2024 data).
- Rural areas: **69.1%** informal employment; Urban areas: **56.9%**.
- The informal economy represents approximately **34.0% of GDP** (~USD $89.3 billion at PPP).
- Informal employment is concentrated in low-productivity activities: street vending, small-scale trade, domestic service, agriculture.
- Workers in the informal economy typically lack access to formal banking, creating a large **unbanked population** that relies on cash and informal value transfer systems.

**Source:** [Statista -- Dominican Republic Informal Employment Share 2024](https://www.statista.com/statistics/1039939/informal-employment-share-dominican-republic/)
**Source:** [Dominican Today -- Informal Employment at 54.7%](https://dominicantoday.com/dr/local/2025/07/27/informal-employment-in-the-dominican-republic-is-around-54-7-according-to-a-regional-report/)
**Source:** [World Economics -- DR Informal Economy](https://www.worldeconomics.com/Informal-Economy/Dominican%20Republic.aspx)

### 6.3 Free Trade Zones (Zonas Francas)

- DR established its free trade zone (FTZ) regime in **1970**, with companies enjoying exemptions from profit taxes, VAT, and import/export tariffs.
- **Gold laundering** through FTZs is a specific, documented risk. DR is both a gold producer and transit hub for gold, much of which passes through FTZs.
- Networks of pawnbrokers are used to launder illicit gold into the legitimate supply chain.
- **Trade-Based Money Laundering (TBML)** is a major concern. DR showed the most rapid growth in potential TBML activity between 2004-2007.
- The international FTZ law has faced criticism for potentially leaving zones outside the AML regulatory regime; implementing regulations have been delayed to address this gap.

**Source:** [OECD -- Free Trade Zones and Illicit Gold Flows in LAC](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2022/12/free-trade-zones-and-illicit-gold-flows-in-latin-america-and-the-caribbean_220669c5/7536db96-en.pdf)
**Source:** [Diaz Estevez -- Free Trade Zones in DR](https://diazestevez.com/free-trade-zones-in-the-dominican-republic/)

### 6.4 Real Estate Sector Risks

- Real estate and construction companies are identified as facilitators of money laundering in the DR.
- Under Law 155-17, real estate brokers are now subject to AML due diligence and KYC requirements, but enforcement is inconsistent.
- **Real estate tokenization** schemes pose emerging risks: the digital, fragmented, and cross-border nature of tokenized real estate makes it difficult to identify beneficial owners, trace funds, and control operations.
- Foreign buyers (particularly from the US diaspora) can purchase property with cash, creating integration opportunities for illicit funds.

**Source:** [INCSR -- Dominican Republic](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/inl/rls/nrcrpt/2016/vol2/253396.htm)
**Source:** [El Inmobiliario -- Real Estate Tokenization Risks](https://inmobiliario.do/en/real-estate-tokenization-in-the-dominican-republic-financial-innovation-or-risk-to-the-dominican-real-estate-registry-system-/)

### 6.5 Remittance Corridors (US-DR Corridor)

- **2024 total remittances: USD $10.756 billion** (5.9% increase over 2023).
- **January-November 2025: USD $10.78 billion** (10.5% increase YoY).
- The **US accounts for 80-83% of all remittances** to DR.
- Approximately **2.39 million Dominicans** live in the United States (out of 2.87 million abroad total).
- Wire transfer remittances are identified as a **primary method** for moving illicit funds from the US to DR.
- Foreign exchange and remittance operations are classified as **high-risk for financial crime** due to high volume and speed of transactions.
- Informal remittance channels (hawala-type systems, couriers) operate alongside formal channels.

**Source:** [Dominican Today -- $10.756B Remittances in 2024](https://dominicantoday.com/dr/economy/2025/01/10/dominican-republic-receives-us10756-million-in-remittances-in-2024/)
**Source:** [DR1.com -- Remittances through Nov 2025](https://dr1.com/news/2025/12/15/remittances-continue-strong-at-us10-7-billion-through-november-2025/)
**Source:** [Inter-American Dialogue -- DR Remittance Market](https://thedialogue.org/blogs/2025/08/the-marketplace-for-money-transfers-to-the-dominican-republic-an-assessment)

### 6.6 Casino and Gaming Sector

- Approximately **44 casinos** operate in the Dominican Republic.
- Historically, the casino sector has **not been adequately covered by AML controls** (per earlier FATF reports).
- Money laundering is described as "common" in activities surrounding DR casinos.
- DR has signed an agreement with FIBA (Florida International Bankers Association) to develop and implement the **Anti-Money Laundering Certification Program (AMLCA-Gambling)** for gaming operators and government officials.
- Recent gambling reform bills have been criticized for lacking solid fiscal controls, oversight mechanisms, and consumer safeguards.
- Compliance costs for AML/CFT updates in the gaming sector are a concern for smaller operators.

**Source:** [iGamingToday -- DR Signs FIBA Agreement](https://www.igamingtoday.com/dominican-republic-signs-agreement-with-fiba-to-strengthen-anti-money-laundering-controls-in-gambling-sector/)
**Source:** [Mundo Video -- DR Gambling Reform Red Flags](https://www.mundovideo.com.co/en/colombian-gambling-news/dominican-republic-gambling-reform-raises-red-flags-a-comparative-look-at-latin-americas-regulatory-paths/)

### 6.7 Drug Trafficking (Cocaine Transit, Fentanyl, Opioids)

#### Cocaine Transit
- DR is a **major cocaine transit hub** between South America (Colombia, Venezuela) and the US/Europe.
- Most cocaine transiting the Caribbean passes through the DR, primarily via **maritime routes**.
- Cocaine is smuggled in commercial maritime vessels from the Caribbean into East Coast ports (Florida, New Jersey).
- May 2018 example: US CBP seized **248 kg of cocaine** hidden in a tomato shipment from DR at New York's Red Hook Port.
- Joint US-Dominican naval operations have resulted in major cocaine seizures.

#### Fentanyl and Opioids
- Multiple DEA cases in 2025 document Dominican nationals involved in fentanyl trafficking:
  - Raynerson Marcos De La Paz Matos: pled guilty to fentanyl and cocaine trafficking conspiracy (Sep 2025)
  - Dominican national pled guilty to trafficking **tens of thousands of fentanyl pills** plus powdered fentanyl, cocaine, and crystal meth (Dec 2025)
  - Dominican national indicted for fentanyl distribution while on supervised release for prior drug conviction (Jun 2025)
  - Four Dominican nationals indicted for fentanyl and crack cocaine conspiracy
- **Sinaloa Cartel connection:** Efrain Sanchez Cabanillas, a Sinaloa Cartel member wanted in the US for trafficking fentanyl, cocaine, and methamphetamines, was arrested at **Punta Cana International Airport** (Feb 2025).

#### Mexico-DR-US Corridor
- The Sinaloa Cartel works with Dominican criminal groups to establish Caribbean trafficking routes.
- Between 2016-2017, a Dominican official allegedly coordinated directly with Sinaloa members in Mexico who supplied heroin and fentanyl, facilitating the importation of **79+ kg of heroin** into New York City.
- Colombian DTOs dominate the New York/New Jersey region, contracting with Dominican and Mexican DTOs to transport drugs from the Southwest Border.
- Drug groups hide cocaine within legal imports and in "mail and tractor trucks" entering from Mexico.

### 6.8 Human Trafficking Risks

- DR is classified as **Tier 2** in the 2025 US Trafficking in Persons (TIP) Report (not fully compliant but making substantial progress).
- **Sex trafficking:** Dominican women and children from impoverished areas are exploited domestically and internationally (Caribbean, South/Central America, Europe, Middle East, US).
- **Forced labor:** Children forced into domestic service, street vending, begging, agricultural work (rice, coffee, tomatoes), construction, robbery gangs, and moving narcotics. Adults exploited in sugar, construction, agriculture, and service sectors.
- **Vulnerable populations:** Homeless children, Haitian children, and migrants from Haiti, Colombia, China, and Asia are at elevated risk.
- **Recruitment methods:** Social media, fake job postings, online romantic relationships, dark web chatrooms.
- **Haitian migrants:** The large population of undocumented Haitian migrants in DR is particularly vulnerable to both forced labor and sex trafficking.

**Source:** [State Dept -- 2025 TIP Report: Dominican Republic](https://www.state.gov/reports/2025-trafficking-in-persons-report/dominican-republic/)
**Source:** [US Embassy DR -- 2025 TIP Narrative](https://do.usembassy.gov/wp-content/uploads/sites/117/2025/09/DOMINICAN-REPUBLIC-2025-TIP-Report-Country-Narrative.pdf)
**Source:** [Wikipedia -- Human Trafficking in Dominican Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_trafficking_in_the_Dominican_Republic)

### 6.9 Facilitator/Transportation Shipments (Mexico to US/NY through DR)

- DR functions as a key **transshipment node** in the Mexico-Caribbean-US drug corridor.
- Colombian DTOs coordinate with Dominican and Mexican organizations to move drugs from the Southwest Border to the Northeast US (New York, New Jersey).
- Methods documented:
  - Cocaine concealed in legal commercial imports (e.g., tomato shipments)
  - "Mail and tractor trucks" from Mexico
  - Commercial maritime vessels from Caribbean to East Coast ports
  - Couriers on commercial flights from DR to the US
  - Puerto Rico used as a US territory staging point
- The New York/New Jersey corridor is a primary destination, with Dominican DTOs operating distribution networks in cities like New York, as well as suburban communities in Westchester County and Long Island.
- DEA extradited a Dominican Republic official in 2020 to face narcotics trafficking charges related to these networks.

## 8. Correspondent Banking Risk

### De-Risking Trends

- The Caribbean region has experienced significant **loss of correspondent banking relationships (CBRs)** since the 2008 financial crisis.
- A 2017 Caribbean Association of Banks survey found that **21 of 23 banks** in 12 Caribbean countries had lost at least one CBR.
- The Financial Stability Board warned that decline in CBRs could become a **systemic issue** for the region and could drive payment flows into the shadow banking sector.
- While the Dominican Republic is less affected than smaller Caribbean nations (due to its larger economy and stronger financial sector), the risk of further de-risking persists.

### DR-Specific Factors

- DR's designation as a **major drug transit country** and major money laundering country elevates risk perception by US/international correspondent banks.
- The large remittance corridor (~$10.8B annually) creates high transaction volumes that require robust AML monitoring.
- Strengthening AML/CFT in line with GAFILAT recommendations should help protect existing CBRs.
- DR's strong GDP growth (5% in 2024) and upper middle-income status provide some insulation compared to smaller Caribbean economies.

**Source:** [CSIS -- Is There a New Normal for De-risking in the Caribbean?](https://www.csis.org/analysis/there-new-normal-de-risking-caribbean)
**Source:** [Caribbean De-Risking](https://caribbeanderisking.com/)

## 9. Red Flags & AML Risk Typology Table

The following table maps specific red flags and risk typologies relevant to Dominican Republic retail banking and small business operations, with references and suggestions for both rule-based detection and anomaly detection features.

| # | Red Flag / AML Risk Typology | Reference Source | Description | Rule-Based Detection (Python Function Concept) | Anomaly Detection Feature |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
