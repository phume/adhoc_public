## 1. Drug Trafficking Routes

### 1.1 DR as a Transit Point for Cocaine from Colombia/Venezuela to US and Europe

**Official Designation:** The U.S. State Department designated the Dominican Republic as a **major drug transit country** for Fiscal Year 2026 under the Presidential Determination on Major Drug Transit or Major Illicit Drug Producing Countries.

- **Source:** [Presidential Determination on Major Drug Transit Countries for FY2026 - U.S. State Department](https://www.state.gov/releases/office-of-the-spokesperson/2025/09/presidential-determination-on-major-drug-transit-or-major-illicit-drug-producing-countries-for-fiscal-year-2026)

**Scale of Transit:**
- At least **120 tons of cocaine** pass through the Dominican Republic every year, a significant percentage of which is destined for Europe.
- Most cocaine transiting the Caribbean passes through the Dominican Republic, primarily via **maritime routes**.
- Approximately **250 metric tons of cocaine** transit through Venezuela each year (accounting for ~10% of global production), with a major portion routed through the DR.

- **Source:** [InSight Crime - Dominican Republic and Venezuela: Cocaine Across the Caribbean](https://insightcrime.org/investigations/dominican-republic-venezuela-cocaine-across-caribbean/)
- **Source:** [Dominican Justice Initiative - U.S. Designates DR as Major Drug Trafficking Hub](https://www.dominicanjusticeinitiative.com/u-s-designates-dominican-republic-as-major-drug-trafficking-hub/)

**Colombia-Venezuela-DR Pipeline:**
- Cocaine enters Venezuela primarily from Colombia through Zulia, Tachira, Apure, and Amazonas states, where production remains under the control of the ELN and FARC dissidents.
- Dominican drug trafficking organizations (DTOs) **buy cocaine in Venezuela** and contract Venezuelans to make the Caribbean crossing, then take direct control of loads once they reach the island.
- Traffickers send go-fast boats laden with up to **one ton of cocaine** from the Guajira and Paraguana peninsulas in Venezuela.

- **Source:** [Transparencia Venezuela - Drug Trafficking in Venezuela 2024](https://transparenciave.org/economias-ilicitas/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/Drug-Trafficking-in-Venezuela-2024.-Transparencia-Venezuela-en-el-exilio.pdf)

**Onward Routes to Europe:**
- From the DR, cocaine is shipped to Europe, primarily through the **Port of Caucedo** (Multimodal Port) via container shipments, with **Belgium's Port of Antwerp** as a major destination.
- In November 2024, 2.2 tons of cocaine were intercepted at the Port of Caucedo, shipped from Panama and destined for Antwerp, Belgium.

- **Source:** [Dominican Today - DNCD Record Cocaine Seizure](https://dominicantoday.com/dr/local/2024/12/06/dncd-makes-record-cocaine-seizure-in-dominican-history/)

### 1.3 Mexican Cartel Connections/Presence in DR (Sinaloa, CJNG)

**Sinaloa Cartel:**
- The Sinaloa cartel has historically sought to work with Dominican criminal groups to establish **Caribbean trafficking routes**, including a route to Europe using the Dominican Republic.
- However, in **July 2025**, the DEA publicly confirmed that the Sinaloa Cartel **does not have an active structured operation** or organic presence in the Dominican Republic. While individuals associated with the group were found in Dominican territory earlier in 2025, their presence did not constitute a structured operation.
- In **February 2025**, a Sinaloa Cartel member wanted in the U.S. for fentanyl and drug trafficking was **arrested in Punta Cana**.

- **Source:** [DEA Confirms Sinaloa Cartel Not Operating in DR - DR1.com](https://dr1.com/news/2025/07/10/dea-confirms-sinaloa-cartel-is-not-operating-in-the-dominican-republic/)
- **Source:** [Dominican Today - DEA Confirms No Sinaloa Cartel Presence](https://dominicantoday.com/dr/local/2025/07/10/dea-confirms-no-sinaloa-cartel-presence-in-dominican-republic/)
- **Source:** [DR1 - Arrest in Punta Cana of Sinaloa Cartel Member](https://dr1.com/news/2025/02/18/arrest-in-punta-cana-of-sinaloa-cartel-member-wanted-in-the-us-for-fentanyl-and-drug-trafficking/)

**CJNG:**
- The Jalisco New Generation Cartel (CJNG) has expanded operations to **over 40 countries** under Ruben Oseguera-Cervantes' leadership. While no specific structured CJNG operations have been publicly confirmed in the DR, the cartel's global reach makes the DR a plausible transit or operational node.

- **Source:** [Global Guardian - The CJNG Cartel](https://www.globalguardian.com/global-digest/cjng-cartel)

**Key Nuance:** The relationship between Mexican cartels and DR-based organizations appears to be more of a **supplier-client or logistics partnership** rather than direct Mexican cartel operational presence on the island. Dominican DTOs maintain **operational autonomy** within the DR.

### 1.4 Maritime and Air Smuggling Routes Through DR

**Maritime Routes (Primary Method):**
- Go-fast boats are the **dominant modality** for moving cocaine from Venezuela/Colombia to the DR.
- Approximately **3 go-fast boats per week** arrive in Dominican waters, each carrying between 700 kg and 1 ton of cocaine, totaling an estimated **115 tons per year** via this route alone.
- Colombians, Venezuelans, and Puerto Ricans work with Dominicans to move drugs via maritime routes to Puerto Rico (gateway to U.S. eastern seaboard) and to Europe.
- **Container shipping** through the Port of Caucedo is a major vector for Europe-bound cocaine.

- **Source:** [InSight Crime - Dominican Republic and Venezuela: Cocaine Across the Caribbean](https://insightcrime.org/investigations/dominican-republic-venezuela-cocaine-across-caribbean/)

**Notable Maritime Interdictions:**
- **CBP Air and Marine Operations (AMO)** interdicted a vessel smuggling 399 pounds of cocaine and 6 aliens from the DR north of Puerto Rico.
- AMO foiled a smuggling attempt in southern Puerto Rico with over **3,000 pounds of cocaine**.
- AMO seized **4,192 pounds of narcotics** near Guayama, Puerto Rico, on a DR-origin route.
- A **12-hour boat chase** in the Caribbean led to the seizure of **1.5 tons of cocaine**.

- **Source:** [CBP - AMO Interdicts Vessel](https://www.cbp.gov/newsroom/local-media-release/amo-interdicts-vessel-smuggling-399-pounds-cocaine-and-6-aliens)
- **Source:** [CBP - AMO Foils Smuggling Attempt](https://www.cbp.gov/newsroom/local-media-release/amo-foils-smuggling-attempt-southern-puerto-rico-over-3000-pounds)
- **Source:** [NBC News - Dominican Republic Cocaine Boat Chase](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/amp/rcna232892)

**Air Smuggling Routes:**
- Historically, drug flights onto Hispaniola increased **four-fold** over a multi-year period, with ~10% of U.S.-bound cocaine shipped through the island.
- Many drug flights originated from Venezuela carrying Colombian cocaine.
- Following successful Dominican air interdiction efforts and dismantlement of two major DTOs in 2010, **drug flights from South America to the DR have largely ceased**.
- In 2025, the U.S. announced plans to **deploy an air base in the Dominican Republic** to strengthen Caribbean anti-drug operations.

- **Source:** [Army Recognition - U.S. to Deploy Air Base in DR](https://www.armyrecognition.com/news/army-news/2025/breaking-news-u-s-to-deploy-air-base-in-dominican-republic-to-strengthen-caribbean-anti-drug-operations)

### 1.5 Shipments from Mexico Through DR to US/NY

- The DR serves as an **intermediate node** in the supply chain. Cocaine sourced from Colombia/Venezuela transits the DR and moves onward to the U.S. via Puerto Rico (then to the eastern seaboard, particularly New York).
- Dominican nationals operate as **wholesale distributors** in New York, Massachusetts, Connecticut, and other northeastern U.S. cities, distributing cocaine, fentanyl, and heroin.
- A Dominican national was sentenced to **16 years** for drug trafficking operations connected to New York distribution networks.
- In 2023, a Dominican national was sentenced for his role in an international conspiracy to distribute **385 kilograms of cocaine** for unlawful importation into the United States.

- **Source:** [ICE - Dominican Drug Trafficker Sentenced to 16 Years](https://www.ice.gov/news/releases/dominican-drug-trafficker-sentenced-16-years-prison)
- **Source:** [DOJ - Foreign National Sentenced for Cocaine Trafficking](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/foreign-national-sentenced-cocaine-trafficking)

## 2. Human Trafficking

### 2.1 DR as Source, Transit, and Destination Country

**The Dominican Republic functions in all three roles:**

**As a Source Country:**
- Dominican women and children, particularly from impoverished areas, are exploited in sex trafficking throughout the DR, the Caribbean, South and Central America, Europe, the Middle East, and the United States.

**As a Destination Country:**
- Foreign trafficking victims -- especially from **Haiti, Colombia, China, other Caribbean and Latin American countries, and Asia** -- are exploited in the Dominican Republic.

**As a Transit Country:**
- The DR serves as a transit point for trafficking victims being moved to other destinations.

- **Source:** [2024 Trafficking in Persons Report: Dominican Republic - U.S. State Department](https://www.state.gov/reports/2024-trafficking-in-persons-report/dominican-republic/)
- **Source:** [2025 Trafficking in Persons Report: Dominican Republic - U.S. State Department](https://www.state.gov/reports/2025-trafficking-in-persons-report/dominican-republic/)

### 2.3 Common Trafficking Patterns and Vulnerable Populations

**Stateless Population Vulnerability:**
- The DR has the **largest stateless population in the Western Hemisphere**. A 2014 law intended to provide citizenship pathways was improperly implemented, leaving **more than 100,000 Dominicans of Haitian descent effectively stateless** and highly vulnerable to trafficking.

**Border Exploitation:**
- Haitian women report that **smugglers often become traffickers** for sexual exploitation along the Haitian-Dominican border.
- Traffickers operate with impunity; **corrupt Haitian and Dominican officials** accept bribes to allow undocumented crossings.

**Children:**
- Children experiencing homelessness and Haitian children are at higher risk of exploitation in **forced labor, including forced criminality**.

- **Source:** [2024 TIP Report: Dominican Republic](https://www.state.gov/reports/2024-trafficking-in-persons-report/dominican-republic/)
- **Source:** [Wikipedia - Human Trafficking in the Dominican Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_trafficking_in_the_Dominican_Republic)

### 2.4 Labor Trafficking in Agriculture and Construction

**Construction Sector:**
- A 2009 NGO study found that of ~500 male Haitian construction workers interviewed, **21% reported experiencing forced labor** in the DR.
- Construction work is the **second most important labor sector** for Haitian migrant men (after agriculture).
- Conditions include: pay far below minimum wage, longer working hours, no days off, and **dangerous and exploitative conditions**.
- A 2008 Solidarity Center survey found **94% of Haitian construction workers were undocumented** and **82% reported paying bribes** to enter the country.

**Agriculture Sector (Sugar Industry):**
- Adults and children are exploited in **forced labor in the sugar sector**.
- Indicators of forced labor include: deceptive recruitment, forced overtime, and withholding of documents.

**Contributing Factors:**
- The precarious legal status of Haitian migrants and Dominicans of Haitian descent -- many unable to access identity documents and work permits -- worsened after a canal border dispute and increased gang violence in Haiti, further increasing trafficking risks.

- **Source:** [Verite - Research on Indicators of Forced Labor in DR Sugar Sector](https://verite.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/Research-on-Indicators-of-Forced-Labor-in-the-Dominican-Republic-Sugar-Sector_9.18.pdf)
- **Source:** [Academia.edu - Haitian Construction Workers in DR: Exploratory Study on Forced Labor](https://www.academia.edu/26596000/Haitian_Construction_Workers_in_the_Dominican_Republic_An_Exploratory_Study_on_Indicators_of_Forced_Labor)
- **Source:** [Destiny Rescue - Terror in Haiti Fuels Labor Trafficking in the Dominican](https://www.destinyrescue.org/blog/terror-in-haiti-fuels-labour-trafficking-in-the-dominican/)
- **Source:** [U.S. DOL - Dominican Republic Child Labor Report](https://www.dol.gov/sites/dolgov/files/ILAB/child_labor_reports/tda2018/Dominican%20Republic.pdf)

## 3. Trade-Based Money Laundering (TBML)

### 3.1 Free Trade Zone (FTZ) Exploitation

- The Dominican Republic has an **extensive FTZ system** that facilitates its role as a Caribbean trade hub.
- Large quantities of **gold -- jewelry, scrap, dore, and bullion** -- pass through the country's FTZs, often as part of the sizeable Dominican jewelry industry.
- FATF has identified that the **lack of AML/CFT safeguards related to TBML** poses a particular vulnerability in FTZs globally, and the DR's FTZs are specifically flagged.

- **Source:** [FATF - Money Laundering Vulnerabilities of Free Trade Zones](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/content/dam/fatf-gafi/reports/ML%20vulnerabilities%20of%20Free%20Trade%20Zones.pdf)
- **Source:** [OECD - Free Trade Zones and Illicit Gold Flows in Latin America and the Caribbean](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2022/12/free-trade-zones-and-illicit-gold-flows-in-latin-america-and-the-caribbean_220669c5/7536db96-en.pdf)

### 3.2 Over/Under Invoicing Patterns

- The **under-invoicing of imports and exports** by Dominican businesses is a "relatively common practice" for those seeking to avoid taxes and customs fees.
- The Dominican Republic, followed closely by Venezuela, was identified by FinCEN as the country with the **most rapid growth in potential TBML activity** based on analysis of Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs).
- TBML techniques include: under/over-invoicing, phantom shipments, and multiple invoicing of goods to justify cross-border value transfers.

- **Source:** [FinCEN Advisory FIN-2010-A001](https://www.fincen.gov/resources/advisories/fincen-advisory-fin-2010-a001)
- **Source:** [U.S. State Department - Dominican Republic INCSR 2016](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/inl/rls/nrcrpt/2016/vol2/253396.htm)

### 3.3 Black Market Peso Exchange (BMPE) Connections

- The **Colombian Black Market Peso Exchange** is a well-documented TBML method that has operational linkages to the DR.
- Similar black market exchange systems are used in the broader Caribbean region, with the DR serving as a node due to its position as a trade hub and its large informal economy.
- Currency exchange houses in the DR are identified as a key facilitator for laundering illicit funds once they enter the country.

- **Source:** [Financial Crime Academy - Black Market Peso Exchange](https://financialcrimeacademy.org/black-market-peso-exchange-money-laundering/)
- **Source:** [U.S. State Department - Dominican Republic INCSR 2016](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/inl/rls/nrcrpt/2016/vol2/253396.htm)

### 3.4 Import/Export Fraud Schemes

- **ICE Homeland Security Investigations (HSI)** has highlighted TBML as a priority area, with the February 2025 Cornerstone bulletin specifically addressing TBML.
- The DR's ports (particularly **Caucedo Multimodal Port**) handle significant container volumes, providing cover for phantom shipments and invoice manipulation.
- The DR's jewelry and precious metals trade through FTZs provides a pathway for **gold-based laundering** -- converting drug proceeds into gold, processing through FTZ entities, and exporting as legitimate trade.

- **Source:** [ICE Cornerstone Report - TBML, February 2025](https://www.ice.gov/doclib/cornerstone/pdf/cornerstone202502.pdf)

## 4. Money Laundering Typologies Specific to DR

### 4.1 Real Estate Purchases

- **Real estate and construction companies** are explicitly identified by the U.S. State Department as facilitators for laundering illicit funds in the Dominican Republic.
- The DR's booming real estate market (particularly in Santo Domingo, Punta Cana, and coastal resort areas) provides opportunities for integration of illicit proceeds.
- The sector is specifically listed as a vulnerable DNFBP (Designated Non-Financial Business and Profession) in FATF/GAFILAT evaluations of the DR.

- **Source:** [U.S. State Department INCSR - Dominican Republic](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/inl/rls/nrcrpt/2016/vol2/253396.htm)
- **Source:** [FATF/GAFILAT Mutual Evaluation Report - Dominican Republic 2018](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/content/dam/fatf-gafi/fsrb-mer/GAFILAT-MER-Dominican-Republic-2018.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf)

The following sectors are identified as laundering vehicles in the DR:

| **Car Dealerships** | Identified as contributing to ML activities | [U.S. State Department INCSR](https://2009-2017.state.gov/j/inl/rls/nrcrpt/2016/vol2/253396.htm) |

**Note on Colmados:** While colmados (small corner stores) are not explicitly named in the searched sources as laundering vehicles, the DR's **large informal economy** -- of which colmados are a major component -- is identified as "a significant market for illicit or smuggled goods" and a vulnerability factor for money laundering.

### 4.3 Smurfing/Structuring Patterns

- Criminal organizations reportedly use money remitters to send money to the DR in **amounts under $10,000** to avoid Bank Secrecy Act reporting thresholds.
- **FinCEN Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs)** were issued against money remitters in the New York Metropolitan area and Puerto Rico, requiring reporting on cash remittances of **$750 or more** to the DR.
- The targeted money remitters in New York sent **more than $500 million to the DR each year**, of which a significant amount was believed to be drug profits.
- FinCEN noted that the **demographics of the New York Dominican community did not support the volume or increments of funds** remitted through the targeted money remitters, indicating structuring.

- **Source:** [FinCEN - Treasury Cracks Down on Remittances to Dominican Republic](https://www.fincen.gov/news/news-releases/treasury-cracks-down-remittances-dominican-republic)
- **Source:** [U.S. Treasury - Press Release on Remittance GTOs](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/rr1904)

### 4.4 Use of Shell Companies

**Velazquez-Cordero Network (the "El Pequeno" case):**
- Luis Velazquez-Cordero led a money laundering organization that used shell corporations to convert **over $80 million** in cash from illegal drug sales.
- The scheme involved converting cash into **cashier's checks at U.S. banks**, depositing checks into accounts controlled by shell corporations, and wiring funds back to DTOs in the DR and other countries.
- The indictment includes a **forfeiture money judgment for $80 million**.
- Velazquez-Cordero was sentenced to **78 months in federal prison**.

- **Source:** [DOJ - Leader of International Money Laundering Organization Arraigned](https://www.justice.gov/usao-nj/pr/leader-international-money-laundering-organization-dominican-republic-arraigned-federal)
- **Source:** [DOJ - Citizen of DR Admits Role as Leader of International ML Organization](https://www.justice.gov/usao-nj/pr/citizen-dominican-republic-admits-role-leader-international-money-laundering-organization)
- **Source:** [DOJ - Citizen of DR Sentenced to 78 Months](https://www.justice.gov/usao-nj/pr/citizen-dominican-republic-sentenced-78-months-prison-role-leader-international-money)

### 4.5 Remittance Abuse

**Primary Methods for Moving Illicit Funds:**
- The **smuggling of bulk cash by couriers** and the **use of wire transfer remittances** are the two primary methods for moving illicit funds from the United States into the Dominican Republic.

**Regulatory Response:**
- Law No. 155-17 (enacted 2017) is the cornerstone of the DR's AML/CFT framework, imposing KYC requirements on financial institutions, foreign exchange brokers, and remittance operators.
- The Dominican Association of Foreign Exchange Brokers (ADOCAMBIO) has partnered with compliance officers to implement measures targeting financial crime.
- In 2025, enhanced AML controls were being implemented for Dominican foreign exchange operations.

- **Source:** [Fincrime Central - Enhancing AML Controls in Dominican Foreign Exchange (2025)](https://fincrimecentral.com/aml-foreign-exchange-dominican-republic-2025/)
- **Source:** [FATF - Dominican Republic Country Page](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/countries/detail/Dominican-Republic.html)

### 4.6 Professional Money Laundering Networks

**The Peralta DTO ($260M+ Operation):**
- Cesar Emilio Peralta ("El Abusador") and his DTO laundered **more than $260 million in drug proceeds** during a three-year period (more than **$7 million per month**).
- The network coordinated transshipment of **ton-quantities of cocaine** and significant quantities of opioids through the DR to the U.S., Puerto Rico, and Europe.
- The DTO also coordinated shipment of **bulk currency proceeds** from narcotics trafficking activities in the United States back to the DR, laundering through a series of nightclubs in Santo Domingo through front persons.

- **Source:** [Treasury - Peralta DTO Kingpin Designation](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sm755)
- **Source:** [InSight Crime - Drug Ring Exposes Massive Money Laundering in DR](https://insightcrime.org/news/brief/dominican-republic-massive-money-laundering/)
- **Source:** [DOJ - Dominican Drug Lord Pleads Guilty](https://www.justice.gov/usao-pr/pr/dominican-drug-lord-pleads-guilty-importing-tons-cocaine-united-states)

## 5. Recent Enforcement Actions

### 5.1 Notable AML/Drug Cases (2020-2025)

#### Case: Cesar Emilio Peralta - "El Abusador" (Ongoing since 2019)
- **Organization:** Peralta Drug Trafficking Organization
- **OFAC Designation:** August 2019 under the Kingpin Act; 8 additional individuals and 6 entities (nightclubs) designated
- **Arrest:** December 2, 2019, in Cartagena, Colombia
- **Extradition:** December 2021, to Puerto Rico
- **Guilty Plea:** Pled guilty to conspiracy to import cocaine and heroin into the U.S.
- **Potential Sentence:** 10 years to life imprisonment
- **Scale:** Multi-ton cocaine shipments from Colombia/Venezuela; $260M+ laundered over 3 years
- **Sources:**
  - [Treasury - Peralta Kingpin Designation](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sm755)
  - [DOJ - Dominican Drug Lord Pleads Guilty](https://www.justice.gov/usao-pr/pr/dominican-drug-lord-pleads-guilty-importing-tons-cocaine-united-states)
  - [Gangsters Inc. - El Abusador Profile](https://gangstersinc.org/2022/03/21/el-abusador-profile-of-dominican-drug-lord-cesar-emilio-peralta-adamez-alleged-to-be-behind-hit-on-mlb-star-david-ortiz/)

#### Case: Luis Velazquez-Cordero - "El Pequeno" (2020-2022)
- **Organization:** International money laundering network
- **Arrest:** July 28, 2020, in the Dominican Republic; extradited to U.S.
- **Guilty Plea:** May 4, 2022
- **Sentence:** 78 months in federal prison
- **Scale:** $80 million laundered through shell corporations and cashier's checks
- **Method:** Cash drug proceeds converted to cashier's checks, deposited into shell company accounts, wired to DTOs in DR
- **Sources:**

### 5.4 FATF/GAFILAT AML Framework Assessment

**2018 Mutual Evaluation:**
- The DR was evaluated by GAFILAT in January 2018.
- **14 of 40 FATF Recommendations rated Compliant**; 20 rated Largely Compliant.
- Key deficiency: Except for banking and securities sectors, there are **important challenges in CDD implementation and risk-based approach**, particularly in DNFBP sectors.
- Most supervisors (outside banking/securities) were only beginning to implement risk-based supervision.

**2019 Follow-Up:**
- Recommendation 18 re-rated from Partially Compliant to Largely Compliant.
- DR remained in **enhanced follow-up process**.

**2017 AML Law (Law No. 155-17):**
- Cornerstone of current AML/CFT framework.
- Imposes KYC obligations on financial institutions, foreign exchange brokers, and remittance operators.
- Covers TBML, beneficial ownership, and suspicious transaction reporting.

- **Source:** [FATF - Dominican Republic Mutual Evaluation 2018](https://www.fatf-gafi.org/en/publications/Mutualevaluations/Mer-dominican-republic-2018.html)
